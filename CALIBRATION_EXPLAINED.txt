CALIBRATION — CONCEPTS AND PROCEDURE
=====================================
Companion to: calibrateIntrinsics.m, calibrateExtrinsics.m, validateCalibration.m


WHY CALIBRATION IS SPLIT INTO TWO STAGES
-----------------------------------------
A camera has two completely separate sets of geometric properties:

  INTRINSICS — properties of the lens and sensor itself.
    How does this particular camera convert a 3D ray in space into a 2D pixel?
    This is determined by focal length (how strong the lens is), the principal
    point (where the lens axis hits the sensor), and lens distortion (how much
    the lens bends straight lines). These do NOT change unless you physically
    adjust the lens or change the resolution setting. You calibrate intrinsics
    once per camera, indoors, and never again unless something changes.

  EXTRINSICS — where the camera sits in the world and which way it points.
    These change every time you move or re-aim a camera. You calibrate
    extrinsics every time the cameras are repositioned.

Separating these is important for your setup because:
  - The checkerboard can be shown to one camera at a time (intrinsics).
  - Extrinsics are recovered from the shared scene the cameras already see
    (the buildings across the street), so no physical props are needed across
    the 10m balcony-to-balcony gap.


INTRINSIC CALIBRATION — WHAT IS HAPPENING MATHEMATICALLY
----------------------------------------------------------
The camera is modelled as a pinhole: all light passes through a single point
(the "optical centre") before hitting the sensor. In this model, a 3D world
point P = [X, Y, Z] projects to pixel [u, v] via:

    [u]       [fx   0  cx] [X]
    [v]  =  1/Z * [ 0  fy  cy] [Y]
    [1]       [ 0   0   1] [Z]

where:
  fx, fy = focal length in pixels (usually nearly equal for square pixels)
  cx, cy = principal point — the pixel coordinates of the optical axis centre
            (usually close to but not exactly the image centre)

Real lenses also distort the image radially — straight lines appear curved
near the edges. This is modelled by two radial distortion coefficients k1, k2:

    r² = (u - cx)² + (v - cy)²
    u_distorted = u * (1 + k1*r² + k2*r⁴)
    v_distorted = v * (1 + k1*r² + k2*r⁴)

MATLAB's estimateCameraParameters recovers fx, fy, cx, cy, k1, k2 by:
  1. You show the camera a checkerboard at many positions and angles.
  2. MATLAB detects the exact pixel coordinates of every interior corner.
  3. It knows what those corners' real-world 3D coordinates are (from
     squareSizeM and boardSize), because a flat checkerboard's corners
     are on a regular grid.
  4. It finds the parameter values that best explain all the detections
     simultaneously — a nonlinear least-squares problem solved internally.

The "reprojection error" is the average distance, in pixels, between where
MATLAB thinks the corners should appear (given its parameter estimate) and
where they actually appear. Below 0.5px is excellent; below 1.0px is usable.

PRACTICAL TIPS FOR GOOD INTRINSIC CALIBRATION:
  - Cover the whole frame — captures in only the centre miss distortion at edges.
  - Tilt the board at varied angles (30-45 degrees in multiple directions).
  - Vary the distance — close (0.3m) and far (1.0m) both.
  - Keep the board flat and rigid (tape it to a clipboard or thin hardboard).
  - A 6x8 interior corner board (7x9 squares) printed on A3 is ideal.
    A4 works but gives fewer corners and slightly lower accuracy.
  - If several captures have much higher error than the mean, they are
    blurry or the board was not flat. Recollect just those captures.


EXTRINSIC CALIBRATION — WHAT IS HAPPENING MATHEMATICALLY
----------------------------------------------------------
Once intrinsics are known, the extrinsic problem is: given two cameras
pointing at the same scene, how are they positioned and oriented relative
to each other?

The answer lives in two 3x3 matrices per camera pair:
  R — rotation matrix: describes orientation (which way the camera points)
  t — translation vector: describes position (where the camera centre is)

Together [R|t] is called the "camera pose". Camera 1 is defined as the
world origin: R{1} = identity matrix, t{1} = [0,0,0].

HOW WE RECOVER EXTRINSICS FROM FEATURE MATCHES:

Step 1 — Feature detection (detectSURFFeatures):
  SURF (Speeded-Up Robust Features) finds distinctive textured regions in
  an image — window corners, tree edges, roof lines. Each detected point
  gets a numerical descriptor (a 64-element vector) describing the local
  texture pattern around it.

Step 2 — Feature matching (matchFeatures):
  For each descriptor in camera 1's frame, find the most similar descriptor
  in camera 2's frame. If the best match is significantly better than the
  second-best (ratio test, MaxRatio=0.6), it is kept. This rejects ambiguous
  matches that could correspond to any of several similar-looking regions.

Step 3 — Fundamental matrix estimation (estimateFundamentalMatrix with RANSAC):
  The fundamental matrix F is a 3x3 matrix that encodes the geometric
  relationship between two cameras. For any pair of correctly matched points
  p1 (camera 1) and p2 (camera 2), the following holds exactly:

      p2' * F * p1 = 0

  In practice, some matches from Step 2 are wrong (outliers). RANSAC handles
  this by repeatedly:
    a) picking 8 random matches,
    b) fitting F to those 8 points,
    c) counting how many OTHER matches are consistent with that F (inliers),
    d) keeping the F that explains the most matches.
  After 2000 trials, the best F explains the true geometry of the scene.

Step 4 — Recover pose (relativeCameraPose):
  Given F and both cameras' intrinsics, MATLAB can extract four candidate
  (R, t) solutions. It selects the one where triangulated points appear in
  FRONT of both cameras (not behind them).

  IMPORTANT LIMITATION: Feature matching alone cannot recover the absolute
  scale of t. The translation direction is correct but the magnitude is
  arbitrary (unit scale). This is a fundamental limitation of vision-only
  calibration — you cannot tell the difference between "cameras 2m apart
  looking at a building 20m away" and "cameras 20m apart looking at a
  building 200m away" from images alone.

  Scale is fixed during validation (validateCalibration.m): you triangulate
  a point at a known distance, compare to the measured distance, and apply
  a scale factor. Alternatively, if you measure the exact baseline between
  two cameras with a tape measure, you can fix scale directly by normalising
  t to that length.


SCALE FIXING — PRACTICAL PROCEDURE
------------------------------------
After calibrateExtrinsics runs:

Option A (recommended): Measure baseline physically.
  1. Use your telemeter to measure the distance between camera 1 and camera 2
     precisely (e.g. 2.25m).
  2. The calibration gives t{2} with some unit-scale length, e.g. norm(t{2}) = 1.0.
  3. Apply scale factor: scaleFactor = 2.25 / norm(t{2});
  4. Apply to ALL cameras: for i = 2:N, t{i} = t{i} * scaleFactor; end
  5. Re-save multiCamParams.

Option B: Use validateCalibration.
  1. Place an object at a measured distance D from camera 1.
  2. Run validateCalibration — it reports results.distFromCam1.
  3. scaleFactor = D / results.distFromCam1;
  4. Apply to all t as above.

Option B is more reliable because it checks the full triangulation pipeline,
not just the baseline. Do both if possible.

After scaling, run validateCalibration again and confirm the reported distance
matches the measured distance within ~5%.


CHAINING POSES (3+ cameras)
-----------------------------
With 3 cameras arranged as on your balconies, camera 3 may not share enough
feature matches with camera 1 (large baseline, different far-field view).

calibrateExtrinsics handles this by chaining:
  - Camera 2's pose is computed relative to camera 1 directly.
  - Camera 3's pose is computed relative to camera 2 (which has more overlap).
  - Camera 3's world-frame pose is then composed:
      R{3} = R_rel_32 * R{2}
      t{3} = R_rel_32 * t{2} + t_rel_32

This is standard practice but introduces a small additional error at each
chain link. With only 3 cameras the accumulated error is negligible.

If chaining still fails (too few matches even between adjacent cameras),
the fallback is the checkerboard-at-distance approach: mount a 1.5m x 2m
board on the opposite building's wall, visible to both balconies. At 20m
range, a 40mm square (printed at large scale) gives ~10px per square —
barely sufficient, but workable with a high-quality print.


OUTPUT STRUCT FIELDS
---------------------
multiCamParams is the struct saved to cfg.calFile and loaded by initSystem.

  .intrinsics{i}   — cameraParameters object for camera i.
                     Contains focal length, principal point, distortion.
                     Used in undistortPoints() before every triangulation.

  .R{i}            — 3x3 rotation matrix for camera i.
                     Columns are the X, Y, Z axes of camera i expressed
                     in world coordinates. R{1} = eye(3).

  .t{i}            — 3x1 translation vector for camera i.
                     The world-frame position of camera i's optical centre.
                     t{1} = [0;0;0].

  .capturedAt      — timestamp string, recorded for bookkeeping.

  .sourceFrames    — the raw frames used for extrinsic estimation.
                     Stored so you can re-inspect match quality later.

The fundamental matrices (.F{i,j}) are NOT stored in this file.
They are computed on-the-fly by buildFundamentalMatrices() inside
initSystem each time the system starts, from R, t, and intrinsics.
This avoids storing redundant data that could become inconsistent.


WHEN TO RECALIBRATE
--------------------
Intrinsics:   Never, unless you change the lens, sensor, or resolution.
Extrinsics:   Every time a camera is physically moved or re-aimed.
              Also recommended if a camera is knocked even slightly —
              a 1-degree rotation error causes ~1.7% depth error at any range,
              which at 100m is 1.7m of position error.

Signs that extrinsic calibration has drifted:
  - validateCalibration distance error grows above 10%.
  - Confirmed tracks jump discontinuously between frames.
  - Two cameras systematically disagree on bird position.
